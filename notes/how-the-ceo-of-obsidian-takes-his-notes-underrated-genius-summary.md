# How the CEO of Obsidian Takes His Notes - Summary

This video explains the note-taking system of Steph Ango, also known as Kepano, the CEO of Obsidian. His system looks simple and messy at first, but it is built around speed, flexibility, local files, links, properties, templates, and review rhythms.

## Core Philosophy

Steph's system is based on a few important ideas:

- File over app
- Few folders
- Fast capture
- Heavy linking
- Lazy organization
- Notes as reusable objects
- Templates for structure
- Properties for flexible categorization

The system avoids over-organizing upfront. Instead, it lets notes accumulate naturally, then uses links, backlinks, properties, and Obsidian Bases to reveal structure later.

## File Over App

Obsidian vaults are just folders full of Markdown files. This means the notes are not locked inside the app.

The main benefit is longevity. If Obsidian stops working, the notes still exist as plain text files.

This is central to Steph's philosophy: the files matter more than the software.

## Minimal Folder Structure

Steph uses very few folders. Most notes live directly in the root of the vault.

In his approach:

- Root-level notes are things he wrote or things directly related to him.
- Folders are not the main organizing system.
- Many notes belong to more than one category, so strict folders create friction.

The downloaded template includes folders like `Notes` and `Categories` for clarity, but the video explains that Steph normally avoids relying on those folders.

## Categories Through Properties

Instead of folders, Steph organizes notes with properties.

Properties are metadata fields at the top of a note. They can describe things like:

- Category
- Created date
- People
- Location
- Rating
- Author
- Topic

For example, a meeting note can have a `categories` property set to `meetings`. Then the `Meetings` category note can automatically show all meeting notes using an Obsidian Base.

This allows one note to belong to multiple areas without moving it into a single folder.

## Quick Switching

The system relies heavily on Obsidian's quick switcher instead of file navigation.

Use:

- `Command + O` on Mac
- `Control + O` on Windows

Rather than browsing folders, you search for the note, category, person, meeting, or topic directly.

## Templates

Templates are central to the system. Steph says almost every note starts from a template.

Templates help by automatically adding useful properties, structure, and embedded tables. This keeps capture fast while still making notes easy to find later.

Common templates include:

- Journal
- Meeting
- People
- Movie
- Quote
- Author
- Evergreen
- Monthly note

The video recommends hotkeys for:

- Creating a unique note
- Inserting a template
- Opening today's daily note
- Adding an internal link

## Unique Notes

Steph often creates unique notes with timestamped titles. These notes automatically include creation metadata and tags.

This makes fast capture easy. You can create a note immediately, write the thought, then add a template or properties if needed.

## Heavy Linking

Internal links are one of the most important parts of the system.

The rule is to link the first mention of something important, even if that note does not exist yet.

Examples:

```md
I watched [[Perfect Days]] with [[Aisha]] at [[Vidiots]].
```

This creates possible future notes for people, places, movies, quotes, restaurants, ideas, and events. Over time, these links create a network that shows how thoughts emerged and how they connect.

Backlinks then let you see every place where a note has been mentioned.

## References Folder

The root of the vault is for personal notes, journal entries, thoughts, and evergreen ideas.

The `References` folder is for things outside the self, such as:

- People
- Movies
- Books
- Podcasts
- Shows
- Genres
- Organizations
- Restaurants

For example, a note about a person or movie may start in the root, then get moved into `References`.

## Ratings

Steph uses a rating property from 1 to 7.

The rating marks how meaningful or life-changing something is:

- `7` means deeply life-changing.
- `1` means negatively life-changing.

Ratings can apply to almost anything, including movies, events, books, people, or ideas. A ratings view can then collect all rated notes.

## Composable Templates

Templates are designed to be composable, meaning multiple templates can apply to one note.

For example, a person who is also an author can use both:

- People template
- Author template

This reflects the system's larger philosophy: one note can belong to many contexts.

## Evergreen Notes

Evergreen notes are reusable idea notes.

They turn ideas into objects that can be linked, refined, and reused across many future notes.

For example, a strong insight like `[[Death is sanity]]` can become an evergreen note. Later reflections can link back to it, building a web of related thinking.

Evergreen notes are for ideas worth revisiting and developing over time.

## Other Folders

The vault also uses a few practical folders:

- `Attachments`
  - Stores images, files, covers, and other media.

- `Templates`
  - Stores reusable templates and Obsidian Bases.

- `Daily`
  - Stores daily notes.
  - Steph's daily notes are mostly used as link targets to trace what was created on a given day.

- `Clippings`
  - Stores web articles or saved internet material, often through Obsidian Web Clipper.

## Note-Taking Rhythm

Steph's system includes a loose review rhythm.

### Daily

Capture thoughts, events, meetings, references, and journal entries as unique notes.

Use descriptive titles, templates, properties, and links when useful.

### Every Few Days

Review recent notes and compile important ideas or patterns into higher-level notes.

### Weekly

Create a simple weekly note with a markdown checklist for to-dos.

The system keeps weekly planning intentionally simple.

### Monthly

Review the major ideas, patterns, and compilations from the month.

Write a higher-level monthly reflection.

### Every Few Months

Use Obsidian's random note feature to revisit older notes.

Follow links and backlinks to rediscover past ideas and generate new connections.

### Yearly

Review monthly reflections and answer a larger set of annual reflection questions.

## Main Takeaway

Steph Ango's Obsidian system is powerful because it is simple, flexible, and low-friction.

It does not depend on rigid folder structures or complex productivity workflows. Instead, it relies on local Markdown files, quick capture, properties, templates, links, backlinks, and periodic review.

The system should not be copied as dogma. Its best lesson is to make note-taking faster, lighter, and more connected, while keeping your notes durable and portable.
